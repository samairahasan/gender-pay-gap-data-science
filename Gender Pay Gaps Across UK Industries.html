Gender Pay Gaps Across UK Industries

Introduction

The gender pay gap remains a persistent feature of modern labour markets, reflecting differences in wages between men and women across occupations, firms, and industries. While national-level statistics provide a useful overview, they often mask substantial variation across sectors.

This project examines how gender pay gaps differ across UK industries and explores whether these differences are associated with two key factors: average wages and female employment levels. By aggregating firm-level data to industry level, the analysis aims to provide a clearer picture of structural patterns in pay inequality.

Data

This project combines three datasets:

  • UK gender pay gap data (firm-level)
  • Annual Survey of Hours and Earnings (ASHE) hourly pay data
  • Female employment by industry (ONS)

All datasets are cleaned and aggregated to the level of SIC industry sections, which form the unit of analysis.

Gender pay gap data is averaged across firms within each industry, while hourly pay is taken directly from ASHE industry-level statistics.

Female employment is measured as the average quarterly level across 2024, rather than an annual total. This ensures consistency across time periods and avoids overstating employment levels.

One important data limitation is that some employment categories in the raw data are grouped (e.g., “B, D, E” and “R, S, T”). These are excluded from the final dataset to maintain consistency with single SIC sections.

The final dataset contains approximately 14 industries, forming a small cross-sectional sample.

Descriptive Analysis

Gender Pay Gaps Across Industries

There is substantial variation in gender pay gaps across industries. Some sectors exhibit relatively small gaps, while others show significantly larger disparities.

These differences likely reflect a combination of factors, including occupational segregation, differences in seniority, and firm-level pay structures.

Wages and Gender Pay Gaps

To explore whether higher-paying industries exhibit larger pay gaps, we examine the relationship between average hourly wages and the gender pay gap.

A simple scatter plot suggests a positive relationship: industries with higher average wages tend to have larger gender pay gaps.

This pattern is consistent with the idea that higher-paying sectors may have greater inequality in senior or high-paying roles.

Female Employment and Pay Gaps

We also consider whether industries with higher female employment tend to have smaller pay gaps.

The relationship appears weaker and less consistent. While some female-dominated industries exhibit smaller gaps, this is not universally the case.

This suggests that representation alone may not fully explain pay inequality.

Regression Analysis

To formalise these relationships, we estimate a simple Ordinary Least Squares (OLS) regression:

  • Dependent variable: gender pay gap

  • Independent variables:

    • average hourly pay
    • female employment

The results indicate that:

  • Higher average wages are associated with larger gender pay gaps
  • Female employment has a weaker and less consistent relationship

However, it is important to interpret these findings with caution.

Given the small cross-sectional sample (n ≈ 14), regression results are exploratory and should be interpreted as descriptive associations rather than structural estimates.

The empirical framework is descriptive and explanatory, rather than causal.

Interpretation

The analysis suggests that gender pay gaps are not uniform across industries and may be linked to structural features of labour markets.

In particular, higher-paying industries appear to exhibit greater inequality. This may reflect barriers to progression, differences in leadership representation, or variation in bonus structures and high-paying roles.

At the same time, the limited role of female employment suggests that simply increasing representation may not be sufficient to eliminate pay gaps.

These findings highlight the importance of considering both industry structure and within-industry dynamics when analysing pay inequality.

Limitations

Several limitations should be noted:

  • The dataset is small, limiting statistical power
  • The analysis is cross-sectional and does not capture changes over time
  • Aggregation to industry level masks firm-level variation
  • Some industries are excluded due to data constraints

These limitations mean that results should be interpreted cautiously.

Conclusion

This project provides a simple but informative overview of gender pay gaps across UK industries.

The findings suggest that:

  • Pay gaps vary substantially across sectors
  • Higher-paying industries tend to exhibit larger gaps
  • Female employment alone does not fully explain these differences

While the analysis is exploratory, it highlights important patterns that could be explored further using richer data or more advanced econometric techniques.

Future work could extend this analysis by incorporating time series data, firm-level characteristics, or causal methods to better understand the drivers of pay inequality.
